El Paso Housing Needs Assessment · Data Story

Is overcrowding a housing shortage,
or a cultural pattern?

A faction reviewing the needs assessment argues overcrowded homes just reflect multigenerational households living the way they choose to. We ran the numbers on 12,932 El Paso–area households to check. Four common assumptions, myth-checked below.

SOURCE: ACS 5-YR PUMS · 2019–2023 UNIT: OCCUPIED HOUSEHOLDS, WEIGHTED GEOGRAPHY: EL PASO CITY PUMAS
Assumption 01

“Overcrowding is being driven by multigenerational households.”

FALSE

Once you control for what a household actually has to work with — its size, its income, whether it rents — multigenerational structure adds almost nothing to the odds of overcrowding.

Assumption 02

“Multigenerational living is a low-income cultural coping strategy.”

FALSE

If this were true, multigenerational households should skew poorer. They don't — multigenerational share is higher above the 70% AMI line than below it, and these households mostly live in ordinary single-family homes, not cramped units.

Assumption 03

“Overcrowding is concentrated among renters and lower-income households.”

TRUE

This is where the real signal lives. Renting more than triples a household's odds of overcrowding; falling below 70% AMI roughly doubles them — both far larger, more consistent effects than household type.

Assumption 04

“Tighter housing markets push more households to double up.”

TRUE

Doubling-up rates track vacancy, not the other way around. The PUMA with the tightest vacancy (Horizon City, 0.004%) has the highest doubling-up rate; the PUMA with the most breathing room (Central, 2.6%) has the lowest. Meanwhile the city has been permitting fewer homes every year since 2015.

Bottom line

It's not who's living together.
It's whether there's enough to live in.

Overcrowding in El Paso tracks renter status, income, and household size — not household type. Multigenerational living happens across the income spectrum, mostly in adequately sized homes. Crowding shows up when low income and renting collide with a shrinking supply of appropriately sized, affordable units.

  • FALSE Overcrowding is a multigenerational-household problem
  • FALSE Multigenerational living is a poverty-driven cultural pattern
  • TRUE Overcrowding concentrates among renters and low-income households
  • TRUE Tighter markets and falling production track higher doubling-up

Methods & caveats

Overcrowded = more than 1 person per room (persons ÷ rooms > 1.0), the standard Census/HUD threshold. Multigenerational follows Census's own MULTG definition (3+ generations in one household).

Household-level odds ratios come from a weighted logistic regression (n=12,932 households, WGTP weights); an unweighted robustness check produced the same direction and significance pattern. Not adjusted for full replicate-weight design effects, so treat exact p-values as approximate.

The vacancy/doubling-up correlation is drawn from only 6 PUMAs — directionally consistent with the rest of the evidence, but too small a sample to stand alone.
