El Paso Housing Needs Assessment · Data Story

Is overcrowding a housing shortage,
or a cultural pattern?

One view on the needs assessment holds that overcrowded homes just reflect multigenerational households living the way they choose to. We ran the numbers on 12,932 El Paso–area households to check. Four common assumptions, myth-checked below.

Assumption 01

“Overcrowding is being driven by multigenerational households.”

FALSE

Once you control for what a household actually has to work with — its size, its income, whether it rents — multigenerational structure adds almost nothing to the odds of overcrowding.

Assumption 02

“Multigenerational living is a low-income cultural coping strategy.”

FALSE

If this were true, multigenerational households should skew poorer. They don't — multigenerational share is higher above the 70% AMI line than below it, and 80.5% of multigenerational households live in single-family homes. The likely reason: these households average 1.94 workers per household versus 1.19 for everyone else — more earners under one roof, not more strain on the same income.

Assumption 03

“Overcrowding is concentrated among renters and lower-income households.”

TRUE

This is where the real signal lives. Renting more than triples a household's odds of overcrowding; falling below 70% AMI roughly doubles them — both far larger, more consistent effects than household type.

Assumption 04

“Tight, unaffordable housing markets have more overcrowding — not just more doubling-up.”

TRUE

The cleanest link isn't a market's raw vacancy number — it's what a household can actually afford. Across all 13 metros tested, the highest-income band (400%+ of the poverty line) has the lowest overcrowding rate, without a single exception. Cities with more doubling-up also have more overcrowding (r = 0.84). A market's raw vacancy rate alone, on its own, barely moves with overcrowding at all — the story runs through affordability, not empty units by themselves.

The income gap is stark everywhere it's measured: in El Paso, overcrowding falls from 10.3% below the poverty line to 1.9% above 400% of it. In Fresno, 17.8% → 3.3%. In Los Angeles, 16.4% → 4.3% — though LA and a few other high-cost coastal metros (San Francisco, Honolulu) show a curious wrinkle: households just above the poverty line are sometimes more crowded than those below it, plausibly because deep poverty carries better access to subsidized housing than the near-poor get.

Notice vacancy rate (second number, each row) doesn't track the overcrowded-rate ranking in any obvious order — Miami and San Francisco have some of the highest vacancy in this set and still land mid-pack on overcrowding. It's household income relative to local cost of living, not the raw vacancy number, that explains the pattern. El Paso sits squarely in the middle of the group, not as an outlier in either direction.

Bottom line

It's not who's living together.
It's whether there's enough to live in.

Overcrowding in El Paso tracks renter status, income, and household size — not household type. Multigenerational living happens across the income spectrum, mostly in adequately sized homes. Crowding shows up when low income and renting collide with a shrinking supply of appropriately sized, affordable units.

  • FALSE Overcrowding is a multigenerational-household problem
  • FALSE Multigenerational living is a poverty-driven cultural pattern
  • TRUE Overcrowding concentrates among renters and low-income households
  • TRUE Tight, unaffordable markets track higher overcrowding — across 13 metros, not just El Paso

Methods & caveats

Overcrowded = more than 1 person per room (persons ÷ rooms > 1.0), the standard Census/HUD threshold. Multigenerational follows Census's own MULTG definition (3+ generations in one household).

Household-level odds ratios come from a weighted logistic regression (n=12,932 households, WGTP weights); an unweighted robustness check produced the same direction and significance pattern. Not adjusted for full replicate-weight design effects, so treat exact p-values as approximate.

The El Paso PUMA-level vacancy/doubling-up correlation is drawn from only 6 PUMAs — directionally consistent with the rest of the evidence, but too small a sample to stand alone. The 13-metro panel (Houston excluded — data collection timed out) uses income-to-poverty ratio (POVPIP), a nationally standardized measure, rather than HUD's area-median-income thresholds, which vary by metro and would require pulling area-specific income limits for all 13 places to compare fairly.
